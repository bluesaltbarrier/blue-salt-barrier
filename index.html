Where it started

A strange pattern on an iPhone.

One day, looking at the weather map on his phone, an engineer noticed something odd. The equatorial rainforests showed continuous rainfall while the adjacent oceans showed intermittent rainfall.

That seemed backwards. Oceans have unlimited water for evaporation. Why would rain be more persistent over land?

Tropical rainforest topsoil is shockingly thin — typically 9 to 15 inches. Far too shallow to store an abundance of water. Trees in this environment must receive nearly continuous rain or they die. Evolution would favor any mechanism that lets trees secure their own water supply.

"Have trees evolved to inject something into the air to grab water for themselves?"

A search of the literature revealed the answer: yes.

In 2012, researchers led by Pöhlker et al. at the Max Planck Institute for Chemistry in Mainz, Germany — with Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory collaborators doing the electron microscopy — discovered that fungi living on Amazon rainforest trees eject microscopic potassium salt particles into the air. These salt crystals act as seeds for cloud formation, triggering local precipitation. The trees and their symbiotic fungi are performing continuous cloud seeding — the biological equivalent of the airplanes that seed clouds for agriculture.

The thermodynamic insight

Where the rain goes, the heat goes.

Every gram of water vapor that condenses into rain releases 2,500 joules of heat. The atmosphere is a heat pipe — water vapor carries latent energy from the surface to wherever it condenses, depositing that energy as warmth.

If salt traps rain locally at the equator, it traps heat at the equator.

If the forests are cut and the salt source is removed, that humidity is free to travel poleward, carrying its latent heat to higher latitudes and releasing it closer to the poles.

This is not a greenhouse gas mechanism. It does not change how much heat Earth traps from the sun. It is a redistribution of existing heat — changing where thermal energy is deposited.

Ice cubes in a glass melt slowly in still water. Stir the water and the ice melts dramatically faster — not because stirring adds energy, but because it thins the thermal boundary layer, letting heat conduct through faster. Removing the equatorial salt barrier is like stirring the atmosphere.

The gap

No mainstream model appears to explicitly represent this mechanism.

Hygroscopicity and cloud condensation nuclei (CCN) observations derived from Pöhlker-era work have entered broader aerosol-climate modeling in various ways. What we have not found, however, is a mainstream global climate model that explicitly represents rainforest biogenic-salt warm-rain emissions from the tree-fungi pathway as a dedicated parameterized process. The aerosol fields in most major climate models are dominated by pollution, sea salt from ocean spray, and mineral dust — the biogenic-salt-specific pathway, coupled to vegetation type, is not where our search has turned up an explicit representation.

This mechanism sits at the intersection of four fields that rarely interact:

Tropical Biology
Biogenic salt aerosol emissions linked to rainforest ecosystems were highlighted in 2012 (Pöhlker et al.); we have not found evidence that this specific mechanism has been explicitly incorporated into mainstream global climate-model physics as a dedicated parameterization.
Cloud Microphysics
Giant salt particles enhance rain (opposite of pollution aerosols which suppress it). The distinction is critical.
Global Dynamics
Meridional energy transport carries 5 petawatts poleward. Even a 2% change is climatologically significant.
Deforestation Science
Deforestation studies have focused primarily on albedo and carbon sinks. We have not identified published climate-model studies that explicitly isolate the aerosol-transport consequences of losing this rainforest biogenic-salt source.
The researcher

Not a meteorologist. A rocket scientist who became a systems pattern-matcher.

The lead researcher trained for five years under Robert Truax — the inventor of the regeneratively cooled rocket engine and Jet Assisted Takeoff (JATO), president of the American Rocket Society, and advisor to President Eisenhower on establishing NASA. He earned two NSF grants studying fluid-structural combustion instabilities in liquid-fueled rockets at the UC San Diego Supercomputer Center, and completed a Master's at Stanford in Aeronautics and Astronautics.

That rocket work taught a lesson that kept reappearing: in complex systems, a chemistry problem in one place can travel through a medium and cause damage somewhere far away. Fuel sloshing in a tank feeding back through the turbopumps and into the combustion chamber — driving POGO oscillations and combustion instabilities that can destroy an engine or tear an airframe apart. Process chemistry in one chamber making defects elsewhere in a semiconductor. And — most relevant to the current work — his later microbiome research (patented) showed that vanishingly small amounts of invisible chemicals secreted by gut microorganisms, reaching the brain via the bloodstream and vagus nerve, can be lethal or cause serious disabling neurological disease. The same pattern holds in climate: nanometer-scale salt particles emitted by rainforest trees can dramatically reshape global rainfall, heat transport, and polar ice balance. In both cases the active species is invisible to the naked eye, present in quantities that look negligible by any bulk measure, yet controls the outcome of a system orders of magnitude larger than itself.

He is not a career meteorologist. But he has decades of training in exactly the physics this problem requires: fluid mechanics, heat transfer, two-phase flow, numerical simulation, and — most directly relevant here — plasma physics. Cloud microphysics and plasma physics are closely related kinds of problems mathematically: huge numbers of small particles colliding, activating, and growing, where the overall behavior depends on how often particles meet, how fast they are moving, and whether they cross an activation threshold. A K-salt particle nucleating a cloud droplet obeys very similar governing equations to an ion forming in a plasma — just slower, colder, and electrically neutral. He has worked on problems of this kind across multiple fields.

He recognized the same template in climate — with an inverted polarity. Rainforest trees produce salt that condenses rain and releases latent heat locally at the equator. When deforestation removes the salt source, moisture escapes poleward, redistributing latent heat — disrupting precipitation and heat-transport patterns that have persisted since the end of the last ice age, roughly 11,000 years ago. The common thread: when critical chemistry goes wrong in one place — either by producing the wrong thing or failing to produce the right thing — the consequences show up somewhere far away. Atmospheric scientists have studied each piece of this chain separately. The hypothesis here is just that the pieces connect — and that the connection has been hiding in plain sight, because continuous rain over rainforests has always been the way weather maps look.

The toolkit

Government software. Consumer AI. A laptop.

Every tool used in this research is freely available. The atmospheric models were built with decades of US government and academic investment. The AI assistants are consumer products anyone can access. The hardware is a laptop you can buy at Best Buy.

🌍
MPAS-Atmosphere
Global atmospheric model from NCAR and LANL. 10,242 hexagonal cells wrapping the entire Earth. 55 vertical layers from surface to stratosphere. Free, open-source, funded by NSF.
🤖
Claude + ChatGPT
Claude (Anthropic) built the Docker containers, compiled the models, wrote the Fortran modifications, and designed the experiments. ChatGPT (OpenAI) cross-verified the equations and caught three errors.
💻
A Consumer Laptop
Intel i7-12700H, 64 GB RAM. Each 30-day global simulation runs in 1.5 hours at 240 km or ~9 hours at 120 km. Total cost of hardware: ~$1,500. No supercomputer required.

The WRF and MPAS models represent decades of taxpayer-funded investment by NCAR, NOAA, DOE, and university research labs worldwide. This software was built so that atmospheric science could advance. Consumer AI made it accessible to anyone with the right question.

The software sat unused on a powerful laptop for two years. Then AI arrived, and the rest is history.

Nine phases of discovery

We got it wrong. Then we got it less wrong. Then a second AI found bugs in our fix.

Every misstep taught us something. We share them all because transparency matters more than polish.

Phase 1
WRF Channel Domain
Failed. Boundary artifacts and domain too small. Lesson: regional models can't study global transport.
Phase 2
MPAS v7 — Autoconversion Hack
Forced more rain at the equator by boosting cloud-to-rain conversion. A signal appeared (Arctic cooled 2.5 K in this single run), but salt was applied over ocean too — 5× too broad. We now treat such single-realization polar numbers as noise-dominated, not findings.
Phase 3
MPAS v8 — Prognostic Aerosol Transport
Real aerosol tracking. Salt emitted only from rainforest cells. But Thompson microphysics treated salt as small CCN (Twomey effect) — wrong rain physics. Arctic warmed instead of cooled.
Phase 4
Giant CCN (GCCN) Tracer — Simplified
Dedicated salt tracer modeled as giant cloud condensation nuclei (GCCN — the larger, ≥1 μm subset of CCN that initiate warm rain via collision-coalescence) with coalescence physics. Strongest rain enhancement (+0.19 mm/day). But fixed drop parameters (E=0.6, V=0.25, R=50μm) gave wrong transport sign.
Phase 5
GCCN Tracer — Full Lifecycle
An apparent breakthrough. Drops grow from 10μm through the coalescence gap. Size-dependent collision efficiency (Hall 1980), terminal velocity (Beard 1976), wet scavenging. Transport read −95 TW and Antarctica cooled −1.03 K in this single run — but later work showed both the sign and the polar temperatures of single 30-day runs are implementation- and weather-dependent, so these are not resolved results.
Phase 6
Iterative Debugging Surfaced Three Bugs
Iterative cross-checking of our GCCN physics against Thompson conventions surfaced three errors: a drop-radius reset keeping collectors below 20 µm, a missing air-density factor, and rain-number routing through the wrong pathway. We fixed them, rebuilt, and re-ran April 240 km. The corrected results differ qualitatively from the buggy version — and the fact that implementation details flip signs is itself the central finding.
Phase 7
120 km January with Bug-fixed Physics — Single Run
In this single January run, rain redistributed northward (+0.18 mm/day at 5°N, −0.21 at 5°S), 30°N transport read −211 TW, 30°S +71 TW, and Antarctic temperature was near-neutral (+0.04 K). At the time this looked like a seasonal mirror of the April run — but with one realization per season that pattern cannot be separated from weather, and the seasonal-mirror framework it suggested was later retracted in v4. The Arctic warming +0.88 K despite reduced transport was the early clue that single 30-day runs are noise-dominated — the lesson the corrected ensembles later made quantitative (σ ≈ 100 TW at 30°N).
Phase 8
Pöhlker Baseline-CCN Matrix — Eight-Run Completed 2026-04-22 (v3)
v3-era single-pair sign-flip narrative. A reviewer-anticipating concern motivated this matrix: MPAS's default Amazon aerosol baseline (~4,400/cm³) is 10–20× higher than Pöhlker et al.'s (2012) direct pristine ATTO observations (~100–300/cm³). We ran an eight-simulation matrix (four K-salt pairs across baseline and activation-size states), switched K-salt representation from giant CCN to accumulation-mode CCN (per Pöhlker's actual observations, Dg = 150 nm, κ ~ 0.8), and explicitly disabled ngccn. v3 results: at the polluted baseline, K-salt appeared to suppress Amazon rainfall (−17.1 mm/30-day); at the Pöhlker-anchored pristine baseline with the observation-matched particle size (Thompson l=4, 160 nm diameter), K-salt appeared to enhance Amazon rainfall (+5.4 mm/30-day). All 8 runs passed independent sanity checks. v4 update: the v3 polluted-baseline result is retired in v4 because the Thompson aerosol-aware single-species microphysics framework cannot represent the chemistry of real anthropogenic pollution at the levels v3's "polluted" baseline implied; multi-species K-salt-versus-smoke separation is deferred to a follow-up using MPAS-CMAQ. The pristine Pöhlker-matched configuration (Dg=160 nm) is the one carried forward into the later ensembles — but v5 note: the pristine Amazon ΔP is itself not statistically resolved once ensembled (+2.8 ± 11.5 mm/30-day, p = 0.62), so the single-pair +5.4 mm enhancement is part of the exploratory record, not a finding.
Phase 9 (v4)
Prescribed-CCN 5-Pair Ensemble — RETRACTED
Using the prescribed-CCN methodology of Heikenfeld et al. (2019, Atmos. Chem. Phys.), we ran a 5-pair January ensemble (2022–2026) at 120 km and reported a reduction in northward latent heat transport at 30°N of −80 ± 22 TW, p = 0.0013. This finding is retracted. A post-publication audit found that four of the five initial-condition files were missing the aerosol fields (nwfa/nifa), and the model silently substituted a hard-coded globally-uniform pristine value (~130/cm³ at every latitude) rather than the intended spatially-varying GoCart climatology. The artificial uniformity suppressed the inter-year spread, manufacturing a significance that does not survive correction. See the corrigendum.
Phase 10 (v5)
Corrected January Ensemble — Transport Null
The corrected experiment. We rebuilt the initialization pipeline to inject and then verify the aerosol fields in four zones (Amazon forest cells, non-forest cells, global mean, latitude bands) on every init file before launch, and re-ran all five January pairs. Result: 30°N latent-heat transport changes by −12 ± 101 TW (p = 0.80) — indistinguishable from zero. The transport response is not resolved at this ensemble size; the per-year weather noise (σ ≈ 100 TW) is as large as any plausible signal. What the corrected run does confirm, as a consistency check, is the mechanism's first step at the source: adding K-salt roughly doubles Amazon cloud-droplet number (+79 to +105 %) in every one of the five pairs — the textbook Twomey activation response any aerosol-aware scheme must produce. Downstream, the Amazon precipitation response bifurcates by year (three pairs wetter, two drier — the well-known aerosol-cloud duality).
Phase 11 (v5)
July Seasonal-Robustness Ensemble — Same Null
A second 5-pair ensemble with July starts (2021–2025), same corrected pipeline, run in parallel on a separate machine. It shows the same result: 30°N transport changes by −30 ± 92 TW (p = 0.51), not statistically resolved. The transport null is not specific to one season.
Phase 12 (v5)
Perturbation Ensemble — Pilot Complete, Extension Underway
The decisive experiment. To separate the forced K-salt response from chaotic weather, we re-run a single year many times with tiny (≈0.01 K) differences in the starting state; the average is the forced response, the spread is the internal variability. A 5-member pilot on the 2023 January pair is complete: the forced mean is −28 ± 29 TW (4/5 members negative), weakly in the expected direction but not yet statistically resolved — and crucially, no member reproduces the +159 TW single-run value that originally looked like an outlier, confirming it was a chaotic draw, not a real regime response. We are extending this ensemble to 15 members, checking, then to 30 — the size a power analysis indicates is needed to pin the forced magnitude down.
The numbers

A clean signal at the source, buried by chaos downstream.

Four years of method development across ten implementations of one hypothesis. An earlier headline — a −80 TW reduction in poleward heat transport — was retracted after an initialization defect was found (see the timeline). The corrected ensembles tell a more careful story: K-salt reliably doubles Amazon cloud-droplet number at the source (a consistency check on the mechanism's first step), but the downstream heat-transport response at 30°N is not statistically resolved — the year-to-year weather noise (σ ≈ 100 TW) is as large as any plausible signal. A perturbation ensemble, now underway, is the experiment built to separate the two.

The v3 sign-flip reading (columns 7–10) — partly retired. In these single-pair runs, starting from MPAS’s default “polluted” Amazon baseline (~4,400 nwfa/cm³) K-salt suppressed local rainfall by 17.1 mm/30-day, while at Pöhlker’s clean-air baseline (~150/cm³) the same K-salt addition enhanced it by 5.4–5.6 mm — a sign-flip with the background state. The polluted-baseline half of this is retired in v4: the Thompson aerosol-aware scheme tracks a single aerosol species and cannot represent the real chemistry (sulfate, soot, secondary organics) that a true polluted baseline implies; the proper K-salt-versus-smoke test is deferred to a multi-species follow-up (MPAS-CMAQ / WRF-Chem-MOSAIC). And the pristine-side enhancement is itself not statistically resolved once ensembled: the corrected v5 January ensemble gives Amazon ΔP = +2.8 ± 11.5 mm/30-day (p = 0.62). The qualitative physics — that the response depends on effective CCN activation capacity (number × size × hygroscopicity), not raw particle number — is the part worth carrying forward; the specific single-pair magnitudes are not.

At Pöhlker’s observed particle size (column 9), global circulation responses are modest. 30°N transport changes by only +31 TW (vs. −172 at default size and +136 at upper-bound), and Arctic temperature changes by +0.12 K — near the noise floor of single-realization runs. These are single-realization numbers and are not statistically resolved, so they motivate rather than prove the following point. A hypothesis-level argument: plants and fungi in the rainforest cannot plausibly have been selected by evolution for effects at the poles — Amazon-to-Arctic transport timescales (months to years) are too slow for any polar consequence to feed back on individual-organism fitness. The observed K-salt size should therefore be expected to optimize for local rainfall with modest far-field consequences, and the (single-pair, unresolved) column-9 numbers are at least consistent with that. The larger transport signals in the other columns are most likely a mix of size assumptions that do not match the observed biology and single-realization weather noise.

Arctic surface wind moved in these single-pair runs, but the response is not resolved. Wind speed over the Arctic core (beyond 70°N) decreased with K-salt at both the polluted (−0.20 m/s) and pristine-default (−0.16 m/s) configurations, and was small at the Pöhlker-matched size (column 9). If such a reduction were real, the mechanism would matter — polar ice loss is dominated by turbulent sensible heat flux (proportional to wind speed), so a few-percent wind change maps to ~1–2 W/m² of heat delivery to sea ice. But these are N = 1 numbers, and the corrected v5 ensemble finds Arctic 10 m wind unresolved at N = 5. We flag this as a quantity worth tracking in the perturbation ensemble, not as a current result.

Columns 5–6 show what once looked like a seasonal-mirror pattern. For the GCCN bug-fixed April/January pair, rain direction, 30°N transport, 30°S transport, and Antarctic temperature all flip sign with the season. With only one run per season this cannot be distinguished from internal variability, and the seasonal-mirror interpretation was retracted in v4; the sign-flips are shown here as part of the development history, not as a confirmed pattern.

The 30°N transport numbers range from −211 TW to +153 TW across ten implementations of the same salt hypothesis, with mean ~−31 TW and standard deviation ~126 TW — larger than the entire estimated effect of anthropogenic CO2 on poleward heat transport. Changing a single drop-radius constant from 10 µm to 25 µm flips sign. Changing the baseline CCN concentration from 4,400/cm³ to 150/cm³ changes magnitude and sign. Changing the aerosol size assumption across the Pöhlker-matched lookup options (160 nm vs 320 nm diameter) reverses direction again.

Two things drive this spread: sensitivity to microphysical implementation choices (the knobs above) and internal weather variability between runs. The corrected v5 ensembles and the completed 2023 perturbation pilot now let us separate the two at the transport level. The result is humbler than earlier drafts of this page suggested: the pilot shows a weak, weakly-negative forced mean (−28 TW) buried under large internal-variability scatter (σ ≈ 65 TW) — a forcing swamped by chaotic weather. The genuinely regime-dependent behavior sits upstream, in the Amazon precipitation response (some years wetter, some drier), not in the far-field transport. The perturbation ensemble that measures this is complete as a 5-member pilot and is being extended to 30 members to pin the forced mean down.

Within the most physically complete April 240 km bug-fixed configuration, three signals appeared in the single realization we ran: (1) salt redistributed equatorial rain southward (zonal-mean rainfall +0.62 mm/day at 5°S, −0.31 mm/day at 5°N); (2) Antarctica cooled by −1.26 K; (3) southward moisture transport at 30°S was reduced by −61 TW. These were N = 1 runs, and we no longer present them as findings. The corrected ensembles show the per-realization noise floor is large enough that no single-pair transport or polar-temperature number is defensible on its own; the polar-temperature and “seasonal-mirror” interpretations built on these single runs were retracted in v4. They are retained here only as part of the development history.

The January 120 km bug-fixed run was a single realization too. Its rain redistributed northward (+0.18 mm/day at 5°N, −0.21 at 5°S, opposite of April), 30°N transport read −211 TW and 30°S +71 TW, and Antarctic temperature was near-neutral (+0.04 K). At the time this looked like a seasonal mirror of the April run — but with only one realization per season, that pattern cannot be separated from weather noise, and the seasonal-mirror framework it suggested was retracted in v4. The Arctic warming by +0.88 K in January despite reduced moisture transport was the early clue that single 30-day runs are noise-dominated — the same lesson the corrected ensembles later made quantitative (σ ≈ 100 TW at 30°N).

We make no claim about specific directions or magnitudes here — single realizations cannot distinguish forced signal from weather noise. The community must help us test this properly with ensembles, convection-permitting resolution, and longer integrations.

The lesson

One radius constant flipped the sign of global heat transport.

When a salt particle activates in a cloud, it creates a droplet about 10 micrometers across. At that size it cannot collect other droplets — the collision efficiency is effectively zero. This is the coalescence gap: a size range where condensation has become too slow to grow drops further, and drops are still too small for collisions with neighbors to work efficiently.

Our first GCCN implementation had a bug: the collector-drop radius reset to 10 µm every microphysics timestep. Over one timestep drops only grew to ~20 µm — right at the boundary where collision efficiency starts but is still minimal. The GCCN physics was essentially idling. That version gave us a −95 TW reduction in 30°N poleward heat transport.

Subsequent cross-checking of the Fortran code against Thompson's native aerosol-scavenging conventions caught the radius-reset bug, the missing air-density factor, and the rain-number routing error. We fixed them. Now collector drops start at 25 µm (an "already-activated embryo"), which gives them a realistic collision efficiency. The same hypothesis now gives us +153 TW at 30°N — opposite sign, larger magnitude.

A swing of hundreds of terawatts across the early single-pair implementations. The 30°N transport response went from −153 TW (v7 autoconversion) to +153 TW (April 240 km bug-fix) to −211 TW (January 120 km bug-fix) depending on which microphysics knobs we turned. The transport swing alone exceeded the estimated entire effect of anthropogenic CO2 on poleward heat transport. Reading those numbers individually, you couldn't tell which sign was real and which was an artifact of the implementation choices — or of the particular weather that happened to start on the chosen day.

An ensemble was the right idea — but the first one's headline did not survive scrutiny. A 5-pair January ensemble (Phase 9, v4) appeared to pin the answer down at −80 ± 22 TW (p = 0.0013), and that number became the original headline of this project. It is now retracted. A post-publication audit found that four of the five runs had been initialized with a defective globally-uniform aerosol field — the same pristine value at every latitude — which artificially suppressed the true year-to-year spread and manufactured the tight bound (see the timeline and the corrigendum). Re-running all five pairs with a corrected, realistic background gives −12 ± 101 TW (p = 0.80); a July ensemble shows the same null. The transport response is simply not resolved at five single realizations, because the per-year weather noise (σ ≈ 100 TW) is as large as any plausible signal.

The deeper lesson is the same one rocket engineering teaches. In a high-sensitivity coupled system, no single trajectory is informative on its own — and, as the retracted ensemble showed, even a small ensemble can mislead if the experiment is not verified end-to-end. You need to repeat the experiment under different starting conditions, and check the inputs everywhere they should vary, before you can separate forced signal from chaotic spread. The single-realization runs taught us that biogenic salt is a first-order variable that mainstream models not explicitly representing this rainforest-emission mechanism are probably missing. Pinning down its forced effect on heat transport requires the right tool: a perturbation ensemble that re-runs one year many times to average over the weather. The 5-member 2023 pilot is the first step — it already shows the apparent +159 TW “outlier” was a chaotic draw that no other member reproduces — and it is being extended to 30 members to measure the forced mean.

A note on accuracy

What consumer hardware can and cannot do.

Most of our experiments run at 240 km resolution — each grid cell covers roughly the area of West Virginia. Individual thunderstorms cannot be resolved at this scale. About 90% of tropical rainfall is handled by a convective parameterization (Kain-Fritsch), not the explicit microphysics where our salt code operates. The v4 January ensemble was run at 120 km (40,962 cells, 4× finer than 240 km), but at this resolution tropical convection is still fully parameterized; the v4 result is therefore a higher-resolution single-mesh confirmation, not a convection-permitting test. True convection-permitting resolution is 30 km or finer — well beyond what a laptop can do for ensembles.

For most of our work we had only N = 1 ensemble member per experiment. The atmosphere is chaotic; weather variability at 30 days is large (~2.67 K mean global divergence between our CONTROL and NO-SALT runs in the early single-pair phases). We could not say whether any specific transport number was a real forced signal or one draw from a wide noise distribution. The range of values we saw across different microphysical implementations (−211 to +153 TW at 30°N) was consistent with either interpretation and most likely reflected both — salt couples sensitively to microphysical implementation AND to weather regime.

The corrected ensembles are our honest attempt to address this directly. Five corrected January pairs (2022–2026) and five July pairs (2021–2025), each with cloud condensation nuclei held at observed pristine values over the rainforest for the full 30-day run, give the same answer: the 30°N transport response is not statistically resolved (−12 ± 101 TW in January, −30 ± 92 TW in July). Amazon ΔP, 30°S transport, polar temperatures, and Arctic 10m wind are likewise unresolved at N = 5. What is reproducible is the mechanism's first step at the source — cloud-droplet number roughly doubles in every one of the corrected pairs. Separating the forced transport response from weather noise is the job of the perturbation ensemble now underway.

What we can trust
At the Amazon source, the corrected ensembles reproduce the mechanism's first step in every pair: K-salt roughly doubles cloud-droplet number (+79 to +105 %) — the expected Twomey activation response, a consistency check confirming the experiment applies the forcing as designed. The downstream Amazon precipitation response is real but bifurcates by year (sometimes wetter, sometimes drier — the known aerosol-cloud duality). And the broader take-away from earlier phases stands: salt is a first-order variable whose sign and magnitude couple sensitively to microphysical and weather conditions.
What we cannot yet trust
The 30°N latent-heat-transport response is not statistically resolved: the corrected January ensemble gives −12 ± 101 TW (p = 0.80) and July shows the same null. The earlier −80 TW headline is retracted (initialization defect). Amazon ΔP, 30°S transport, polar temperatures, and Arctic 10m wind are all unresolved at N = 5, and the single-pair numbers in earlier table columns sit within the same wide noise band. None of this is convection-permitting.
What the community needs to do
(1) The perturbation ensemble — re-running one year with tiny initial-state differences — to separate the forced transport response from weather noise; the 2023 pilot is complete and extending to N = 30. (2) Larger or longer single-realization ensembles to push Amazon ΔP and polar metrics into resolution. (3) 30 km convection-permitting paired runs (requires HPC). (4) Multi-species frameworks (MPAS-CMAQ, WRF-Chem-MOSAIC) to test the K-salt-versus-smoke chemistry distinction at high CCN concentrations — the basis of the v3 polluted-baseline result that v4 has retired.

The strongest claim we can defend from current data: the corrected ensembles reproduce the K-salt mechanism's first step at the Amazon source — cloud-droplet number roughly doubles in every pair — but the downstream poleward heat-transport response at 30°N is not statistically resolved (−12 ± 101 TW, p = 0.80), and the earlier −80 TW figure is retracted. Whether the forced transport effect is small-but-real, regime-conditional, or near zero is the open question the perturbation ensemble is designed to answer. The broader implication — that biogenic salt from rainforests is a first-order climate variable worth representing explicitly — rests on the verified source-step response and on the wide implementation-and-weather sensitivity documented across the project, not on any single resolved transport number.

An invitation

This hypothesis belongs to everyone.

We are sharing the code, configuration files, analysis scripts, Docker build recipes, and this entire paper. Raw simulation output (~200 GB of NetCDF) is available on request and planned for a public data archive. We believe this question is too important to sit behind a paywall or wait for a grant cycle.

HPC-enabled climate modelers (highest priority)
Run our GCCN code at 30 km or finer (convection-permitting). A single paired run would settle whether the 240 km Hadley intensification is real physics or parameterization artifact. Tell us what your MPAS or WRF setup can do — we'll help port.
Cluster or cloud-compute access
Run larger perturbation ensembles than we can (20+ members), or longer integrations (90+ days), to separate the forced K-salt transport response from internal weather variability and pin down its magnitude. This can be done at 240 km for much less compute than convection-permitting runs.
Cloud microphysicists
Review the GCCN lifecycle code in our repo. Try the same hypothesis with Morrison or P3 microphysics — which parts of the response are Thompson-specific?
Tropical ecologists
Which tree species emit the most salt? What are the emission rates by canopy density, soil moisture, diurnal cycle? Field measurements inform all future modeling.
Citizen scientists
Download our Docker container and reproduce the April 240 km experiment. Try different seasons. Three days of laptop time produces a publishable replication. AI can help you set it up.
Policy makers and conservation organizations
The magnitude of sensitivity we observe is sufficient reason to ensure rainforests are not destroyed faster than we can understand what we lose. Our results do not yet determine the sign of the polar effect — but neither does "it is probably zero" look defensible anymore.
The bigger picture

When budgets are slashed, citizens step up.

The tools that made this research possible — WRF, MPAS, GFS data, satellite observations — represent decades of investment by the US government, NCAR, NOAA, DOE, NASA, and academic institutions worldwide. They were built to advance science. They are free because taxpayers funded them.

Consumer AI (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini) made these tools accessible to non-specialists. A rocket scientist with a laptop can now ask questions that previously required a team of meteorologists and a supercomputer. The accuracy needs professional validation, but the questions can come from anywhere.

With research budgets under pressure, this model — government tools + consumer AI + citizen scientists + professional oversight — may be the future of climate research. Not replacing institutional science, but accelerating it with a thousand curious minds running experiments that no single lab has the bandwidth to attempt.

Biogenic salt from rainforests appears to matter more than mainstream models explicitly capture through a dedicated warm-rain mechanism. The specific effect on polar ice is still being determined. That uncertainty is not a reason to wait — it is a reason to ensure forests are not destroyed faster than we can understand what we're losing.
